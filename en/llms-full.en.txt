# Ihor "Slon" Utyuzh — In Memoriam

> A memorial website honoring Ihor "Slon" Utyuzh (April 18, 1996 — December 7, 2023) — a defender of Ukraine, UAV operator with the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment, and participant in the Revolution of Dignity. Built by his family and friends in 2024–2026 to preserve his story, his values, and the memory of a man who twice chose to step forward — at 17 onto the Maidan, and at 25 into the full-scale war.

This file is a complete text version of the materials published at https://slon.life/, intended for AI systems and search agents. All texts and quotations are used with the permission of Ihor's family. The tone of the site is restrained, respectful, and documentary. ## 2. Home page

**Headline:** Ihor "Slon" Utyuzh

**Subtitle:** Warrior, friend, son. 18.04.1996 — 07.12.2023

**Description:** A defender of Ukraine, a soldier of the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment, a participant in the Revolution of Dignity. A man who twice chose to step forward — at 17 onto the Maidan, and at 25 into the full-scale war.

**Ihor's defining quote:** *"If my life was saved, then I'm needed for something bigger."*

### Who he was to us

**Childhood and character.** From childhood, Ihor was active, energetic, and curious. His parents remember him as a boy who "always found something to do" and had a natural persistence. He practiced martial arts, rowing, and loved sports and people. His ability to connect with others was almost mystical — a few words, a smile, and he was already "one of us" among strangers.

**Life before the war.** Before the full-scale invasion, Ihor lived the ordinary life of a young man: studies, friends, travel, football, chess, plans for the future. He loved his city, loved watching the national team play, loved simple joys. He dreamed of going to Africa one day — to protect the elephants. Elephants were a childhood love that eventually grew into his call sign.

**Revolution of Dignity.** At 17, Ihor made a choice that defined the rest of his life. He became part of the 24th Maidan Self-Defense Company, stood watch on the barricades, helped pull out the wounded. On February 18, 2014, in Mariinsky Park, he was severely wounded — part of his skull was shattered. Doctors saved his life. Then he said a phrase that turned out to be prophetic: "If my life was saved, then I'm needed for something bigger."

### The warrior's path

**2014–2016. The first war. ATO.** Despite his serious wound from the Maidan, Ihor went through rehabilitation and joined the Anti-Terrorist Operation. He was awarded the medal "For Sacrifice and Love for Ukraine." He came home from the front, but the war never let him go.

**February 24, 2022. The return.** Even before the full-scale invasion, Ihor had told his family: "If it starts — I'm going." On February 24, 2022, he was abroad — and returned to Ukraine by every possible route. He enlisted in the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment. He became a UAV operator.

**2022–2023. Kyiv region, Kharkiv region, the South.** He took part in the liberation of the Kyiv and Kharkiv regions. Later — the Zaporizhzhia and Kherson directions. He worked the hardest sectors, supported his comrades, never left his own behind. In December 2023, while carrying out a combat mission, his position was struck by an enemy FPV drone. Ihor was killed shielding his comrades — just as he had lived: standing alongside them, to the end, without fear and without a step back.

### The symbols of his path

- **Elephant (Slon).** A call sign born from a childhood love, which grew into a symbol of strength, endurance, and the ability to take a hit. "Slon" — the one who moves forward when others stop.
- **Shield.** Protection of comrades, family, home. What Ihor was to the people around him — both in Mariinsky Park and in the trenches of the Air Assault Forces.
- **Ukraine.** What he twice chose to defend — at 17 and at 25. What he lived for, and what he gave his life for.

## 3. Biography (full text)

A remembrance of soldier Ihor Utyuzh, who was killed on December 7, 2023. This text was written based on conversations with Ihor's family: his parents, Oleh and Nina Utyuzh, and his fiancée, Yevheniia Shkliaruk.

### Chapter One. One of our own

As a child, Ihor was restless and full of energy. A bit of a scamp (in the good sense) — eyes burning, certain that nothing was impossible. He was always looking for adventure, and from a young age he was tempering his character: his schedule cycled through karate, jiu-jitsu, and rowing.

With age, an iron character and stubbornness were added to the energy. And so was a love for people. Ihor could unofficially be called a master of the sport of building connections. In his hands it was an art form: a few words, a smile — and Ihor was already "one of our own," a good friend, the kind of person you'd go through fire and water with.

After school, Ihor entered Kyiv Polytechnic Institute on his own merits, on a state-funded place. And in his first year, when he was 17, the turning point came — the Revolution of Dignity began. It shaped him as a citizen and crystallized his values.

> "Something pulled them there. Sometimes they'd skip class, or go straight to the Maidan after class with the other boys. After the students were beaten, Ihor found his place there. At 17, still essentially a child, he understood clearly that he wanted to live in a free, independent country, moving in a pro-European direction and refusing any sign of dictatorship."
> — Nina, Ihor's mother

Ihor was part of the 24th Maidan Self-Defense Company — and that's when he learned what it meant to fight for what's yours. He wasn't yet 18 when the Revolution of Dignity became part of his life: he patrolled the Maidan, stood on the barricades, took part in clashes with the "titushky" and the Berkut riot police, and pulled people out of the hands of security forces.

> "They wouldn't sleep there for days at a time, and if they did manage to rest, it was right there — in tents by the fire, or on the floor of the Kyiv City Hall building. They were still very young when they had to stand without weapons or protective gear against fully armed Berkut and trained titushky with metal pipes. All they had was their drive, their courage, and homemade Molotov cocktails. In the rare moments when Ihor made it home, he came back exhausted and completely black from the smoke."
> — Nina, mother

The hardest days on Hrushevskoho Street, Ihor spent on the front line — he took several rubber-bullet wounds and was gassed. His parents remember the Maidan with fear and pride: during the clashes on Hrushevskoho, a fire broke out in one of the shops. Ihor, on his own initiative, took up the Ukrainian flag and walked out to negotiate with the security forces so they would let the fire trucks through. A silence had been called on the barricades at that moment. Brave, persuasive, and still so young, Ihor came back with the flag in his hands, with the firefighters — and with his first victory.

The events of 2014 sharpened in Ihor a heightened sense of justice and loyalty to principle. From then on, these traits would be his compass, lying at the heart of every choice he made.

> "He was a scamp as a child, so his actions varied. We tried to instill certain boundaries from early on — by showing him examples in books and films. But Ihor and boundaries — those don't go together. As he grew up, Ihor came on his own to certain limits, and those became his conscience, his belief in truth, and his sharpened sense of justice."
> — Oleh, Ihor's father

### Chapter Two. "If my life was saved, then I'm needed for something bigger"

Stubbornness, honesty, courage, drive — those are the traits that first come to mind when we remember Ihor. He was, above all, a Person. With values, depth, and conviction. That's why he was always at the front, moving toward danger rather than away from it — on the Maidan in 2014, during the full-scale invasion in 2022. Between those two events lay eight years. But the virtues that drove Ihor stayed the same.

During the Revolution of Dignity, on February 18, 2014, Ihor was severely wounded near Mariinsky Park. It happened during a clash between unarmed people and the Berkut. For a long time he wasn't given medical help — the attackers kept him at the scene, in a state between life and death. Iryna Herashchenko and her colleagues finally managed to get him to a hospital.

The doctors performed several emergency operations: Ihor was in critical condition — part of his skull had been shattered. In the end, a titanium plate had to be installed, and he went through a long course of rehabilitation. Afterwards, when he had recovered, Ihor said to his parents the words that defined everything that followed:

> "If my life was saved, then I'm needed for something bigger."

> "If he hadn't been beaten then, if he hadn't been in the hospital during the days of the mass shootings on the Maidan, we don't know whether Ihor would have stayed alive, or whether he would have joined the Great War he later took part in…"
> — His parents

Despite the serious injury he had sustained on the Maidan, within a few years Ihor joined the ATO: he went through special training and served on second-line defense duties. In 2016, at the age of 20, he received the medal "For Sacrifice and Love for Ukraine."

### Chapter Three. "The war has started! I'm coming home!"

Three or four months before the full-scale invasion, Ihor sensed that a great fight was approaching. And he already knew that this fight would be his personally. So that autumn of 2021, he told his family: "If this happens, I'll be there, I'll go." Ihor left no room for doubt or hesitation. As with all his decisions in life, his desire to defend the country was unshakable.

Ihor received the news of the Russian full-scale invasion while celebrating the anniversary of meeting his girlfriend in Budapest. The morning of February 24 began with two sentences: *The war has started! I'm coming home!*

> "He called more than 20 transport companies. He only got refusals. No one wanted to go to Ukraine. With several transfers, he made it to the Ukrainian border. From Chop he took a train. Ihor was the only passenger in an empty carriage. Once inside Ukraine, he traveled to Kyiv by car with acquaintances. On the approach to Kyiv they came under fire. But stubborn, he kept going home. Kyiv's Territorial Defense searched Ihor for a long time — they found it strange that this young man was driving toward the city while a column of many thousands was moving West. We often remembered that day and joked about the most expensive taxi ride of his life."
> — Yevheniia, fiancée

For the first two months, Ihor had a cover story: so his family wouldn't worry, he convinced them he was volunteering around the city — when in fact he had immediately joined the defense of Kyiv with ALFA. Later, after the liberation of the Kyiv region, Ihor became part of the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment. But for a long time — unofficially. Even in the conditions of a full-scale war, bureaucracy slowed down the process of formally enlisting him in the Air Assault Forces.

> "Nothing could change Ihor's desire to defend the country. For a long time he wasn't officially registered with the military, so he received no salary — even though he was in hot spots. The guys in his unit decided they'd chip in to give Ihor at least some minimum pay. He was uncomfortable with it. We were terrified. But he always had the same unshakable answer: 'I want to be with the boys. That's it.' The money didn't matter. Neither did the formalities. It was a pure desire."
> — His parents

Calls home from the front line came with phrases like: "Mom, I'm far away, I'm not on the front line! Everything's fine." This was a deception out of care — Ihor knew how worried his family was.

### Chapter Four. "Dad, when I grow up, I'll go to Africa to protect the elephants…"

To his comrades, Ihor was "Slon" — that was his call sign. Because he was built like one: 1.95 meters tall, shoe size 46, with shoulders broad enough to hide a small world behind. Ihor loved elephants. In his school years, he told his father:

> "Dad, when I grow up, I'll go to Africa to protect the elephants. They get killed so often for their tusks."

Elephants, by the way, are among the most intelligent animals. What makes them unique among mammals is their mutual support: they never abandon their own and always stay together as a group. Ihor had this trait too — never leave your people, stay with the herd, with one another, in a single spirit. Friends, brothers-in-arms — these were his second family, one he was proud of. At Ihor's funeral, everyone came with elephants: the defender's grave was covered in colorful toys.

Yevheniia — Ihor's beloved — recalls that over time, the war and ordinary life merged into one. Ihor would call from the trenches, and she would worry that he wasn't wearing a hat or a helmet. Those simple gestures of care and love were what two separated souls held onto. And when, in Kyiv, her heart was clenched with worry, Ihor would say reassuringly: "Don't worry, from us to them it's a hundred meters, look how far the shells land." For him everything was always "okay" — he never complained, never looked for someone to blame (except, of course, the Russians), and carried out his mission with conviction, even when it meant collecting downed drones from a minefield.

The last message Ihor wrote before he was killed was confident and full of life: *"The day starts not with coffee, but with dead orcs…"*

Ihor's commander wanted to see him in headquarters. There, he said, his knowledge and skills were needed. But Ihor was unshakable; he wanted to be with the guys up front:

> "I want to go into cities and plant flags. I want to be there."

Ihor tried to be as easy and as caring with his family as he could — protecting his loved ones with every word and action from the terrible reality. He didn't allow himself weakness or fear, because he understood that he was a support, that those who needed protection most were holding onto him. He felt that this small, warm world — his family — was sheltered behind his broad shoulders.

> "At some point I felt that my son had become the elder in the family. His experience, his decisions, his character. I felt a strength in him that came to lead our family."
> — Oleh, father

Yevheniia recalls that she saw Ihor cry only once in his life: he had just returned to Kyiv on a short leave, and that evening, in the noisy city that kept living its seemingly ordinary life, he spoke of the comrades who had been killed. Those few tears were not a sign of weakness. On the contrary — to release the emotion and to show what was truly on his soul was, for Ihor, a strong act and a revelation.

### Chapter Five. "I'll do everything I can to survive"

Ihor was certain of victory and had a clear vision of what it had to look like — the de-occupation of all Ukrainian territories and a return to the 2014 borders, deeper shifts in public attitudes, an active pro-European stance.

He believed in people — saw the best in them — but he was intolerant of an absence of values, of bribery, of weak character. He knew clearly what was worth something in this life and what was dust. He also knew why he had become a defender, what he was fighting for. He didn't fear death, didn't fear battle. He feared the unknown of captivity, but he said:

> "I'll do everything I can to survive."

Because he wanted very much to see victory.

Soldier Ihor Utyuzh took part in the liberation of the Kyiv and Kharkiv regions, and fought on the Zaporizhzhia and Kherson directions. He was a UAV operator. His last battle was on the Zaporizhzhia front, where Ihor and his comrades were covering an assault brigade.

That morning, Ihor's team had managed to launch 12 drones with a perfect result — 12 strikes on enemy positions. But this also enraged the Russians, who were now hunting the drone team. As the 13th drone was being launched, an enemy round struck the boys' dugout. There was no chance of survival.

But we still have the chance to preserve Ihor's story. A brave young man from Kyiv who loved this life and savored it to the fullest — with countless hobbies, travels, football, and big dreams. After victory, the first thing he was planning was a trip to Crimea. He was preparing a tent, hiking gear, and mountain routes for it.

Before the war, Ihor was a rafting instructor, and he found enormous joy in running wild mountain rivers. One day he wanted to conquer the famous rafting routes in America and Africa. He was obsessed with water and speed. He also dreamed of starting his own charitable foundation.

In February 2022, Ihor made his choice and stepped up to defend what mattered most. Slon gave his life so that there would be victory, so there would be Crimea, freedom, a successful Ukraine — so that our lives could go on, and look the way Ihor loved them: with travel, football, sports, beloved friends, big plans, and even bigger dreams.

Thank you, Slon. Eternal memory to the Hero.

## 5. Stories — entries from the memorial blog

### Happy Birthday, My Son… (April 18, 2026)

*By Nina, mother*

Today is a day that was supposed to be a celebration — my son would have turned 30. We should have gathered together, laughed, joked, embraced the birthday boy, made plans… But instead, we are going to the cemetery to lay flowers and bring his favorite meal.

It is deeply painful to accept that my son, like thousands of our finest, will never again celebrate birthdays, fulfill his dreams, or come home with a smile. He gave his life defending Ukraine from Russian aggression.

Ihor was one of those people who never walked past another's pain. He grew up kind, honest, and responsible — someone you could always rely on. He worked, built his life, dreamed of simple human things. And when the full-scale war began, he did not hesitate. He volunteered because he believed it was the right thing to do. He served in a unit that carried out the most difficult missions, and he did so with the inner dignity that was always a part of him.

His brothers-in-arms recall that he never hid behind others, always went first, and always supported those who were struggling. He was the kind of person who held others up — and held Ukraine up.

Now he is one of our Heavenly Guardians.

And today I want people to remember him. To know how bright, kind, and strong he was. To know the price we all pay.

Eternal memory to my son. Eternal memory to all who did not return.

### A memorial plaque at Secondary School No. 277 (May 2, 2024)

*By Oleh, father*

Today, Secondary School No. 277 unveiled a memorial plaque honoring Ihor Utyuzh — a graduate whose journey began within these walls. Here he found his first true friends, the people who later stood with him on the Maidan during the Revolution of Dignity, and who remained by his side when he chose to go to war.

This school shaped the character that led Ihor to defend his values, first on the barricades and later on the frontline. His path — from these classrooms to the Maidan and then to the battlefield — is a reminder of how courage grows from ordinary days and familiar places.

The memorial plaque is not only a tribute to his memory. It is a reminder that heroes often start as students walking these same corridors.

Eternal honor to Ihor "Slon." His story began here — and here it will live on.

### The concert Ihor finally made it to (February 7, 2024)

*Told by Yevheniia, the fiancée*

There are moments that are difficult to put into words. Moments when the line between the world of the living and the world of those we've lost feels almost transparent. That is exactly what happened that evening in Kalush, at the concert of "Zhadan and the Dogs."

Ihor loved their music deeply. Especially the song "Troieshchyna." For him, it wasn't just a track — it was a talisman. Before every mission, he would smile and tell his brothers-in-arms: "Everything will be fine, because I was baptized in the church on Troieshchyna."

He dreamed of attending their concert. And we did everything we could to make that dream come true — in our own way.

We brought a large photograph of him and bought a separate seat in the first row. As if Ihor were sitting right there with us. As if he had just stepped away for a moment and would return any second.

When the concert began, people moved closer to the stage, and Ihor's portrait ended up behind the crowd. And then something happened that we will remember forever. One of the musicians came over, gently picked up Ihor's photo — and placed it right on the stage. In the spotlight. Next to the band he loved so much. Exactly where he should have been standing himself.

Before performing "Troieshchyna," Serhiy Zhadan said a few warm words about Ihor — about his story, his strength, and about all our defenders who gave their lives for Ukraine. They dedicated this song to him.

After the concert, we met with the band. We talked. They signed Ihor's photo, gave us gifts with the band's symbols, and spoke with sincerity and kindness. There were no formalities — only gratitude, memory, and warmth.

We are grateful to Serhiy Zhadan for his words. Grateful to the entire "Zhadan and the Dogs" team for their support and humanity. Grateful to Kalush — a small but incredibly bright city.

That evening, Ihor was with us. And we felt it.
